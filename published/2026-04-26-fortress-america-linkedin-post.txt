There is an irony in global trade right now so precise it reads like satire.

The United States — the country that designed, funded, and enforced the postwar free-trade order — is now the country tearing it down. Meanwhile, the nations that spent most of the twentieth century as protectionist holdouts are signing trade deals faster than at any point in modern history.

The roles have completely swapped.

Most people reach for the Smoot-Hawley analogy: America raises tariffs, everyone retaliates, trade collapses. But the 2025-2026 story is fundamentally different from 1930 in one crucial way.

In 1930, American protectionism was contagious. Countries didn't just retaliate against the US — they raised tariffs against each other. The world shattered into hostile blocs. Trade collapsed by two-thirds.

In 2026, retaliation against the US is real and substantial. China pushed rates to 125%. Canada hit US steel and aluminum. The EU reactivated countermeasures. But outside those US-targeted responses, the disease is not spreading.

In fact, the opposite is happening.

Analysts call it the "n-2" pattern — everyone except the US and China is deepening trade with each other. The EU signed with Mercosur after 25 years of stalemate. India signed with the UK, the EU, EFTA, and New Zealand. Canada reached deals with India, Indonesia, Ecuador, and the UAE. The EU and CPTPP launched a formal trade dialogue. Together, these "n-2" countries account for 75% of world trade.

Trump's tariffs didn't trigger global protectionism. They triggered global liberalization — providing the political cover for deals that had been stuck for decades.

The deeper damage isn't the tariffs themselves. It's the rule-setting loss. For ninety years, the US wrote the rules of global trade. GATT, WTO, every major multilateral framework was American-designed. That table is being rebuilt right now — without an American seat.

Once those new rule frameworks are ratified, the US doesn't get its chair back by simply lowering tariffs. It becomes a rule-taker in the system it used to design.

The pattern is familiar to anyone who studies networks: when a dominant node starts taxing traffic through itself, the network routes around the bottleneck. The routing takes time. But once the alternative pathways are established, the traffic doesn't come back. It has no reason to.
