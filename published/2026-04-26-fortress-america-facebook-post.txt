The United States built the postwar free-trade order. Now it's the country dismantling it — while the rest of the world races to liberalize without it.

The 1930 Smoot-Hawley analogy keeps getting invoked, but 2026 is fundamentally different. In 1930, protectionism was contagious — everyone raised tariffs against everyone. In 2026, countries are retaliating against the US, but between themselves they're signing more trade deals than at any point in modern history.

Analysts call it the "n-2" pattern. Everyone except the US and China is deepening trade with each other — representing 75% of world trade.

The real damage isn't the tariffs. It's the rule-setting loss. Once those new frameworks are ratified, the US becomes a rule-taker in the system it used to design.
