Everything is getting harder to buy — but only if you're not a consumer.

That's the part nobody talks about. In the B2C world, buying has never been easier. One-click checkout, same-day delivery, AI-powered recommendations. Consumer friction has been engineered almost to zero. The platforms that did this are genuine marvels.

But step into the B2B world, and the story flips completely.

In 1990, if you needed an industrial pump, you called a distributor, gave them a flow rate, and they shipped one. Today that pump has IoT monitoring, cybersecurity compliance, jurisdictional energy certifications, integration APIs, and a service contract with remote diagnostics. The pump is better. The act of buying it is dramatically harder.

I call this the complexity ratchet. Technology improves the product but raises the knowledge barrier to trading it. And it only turns one way.

Every generation of advancement adds a layer — software integration, environmental certification, regulatory compliance, cybersecurity requirements — that never gets removed. The next generation doesn't simplify. It stacks another layer on top.

The consequence is that B2B markets are systematically thinning. Not because supply or demand dried up, but because the knowledge required to close a deal grew faster than the institutions that facilitate deals could adapt. Standards bodies run on multi-year cycles. Industry platforms standardize around the last generation of complexity. By the time they catch up, the products have moved on.

This creates a distributional problem nobody tracks. Large firms with procurement teams, legal departments, and regulatory affairs can navigate complexity. Small firms cannot. A 50-person manufacturer making an excellent specialized component may never find its optimal customers — because the search and evaluation friction exceeds what a small commercial team can bear.

The irony: the same tech companies that simplified consumer buying — Amazon, Google, Shopify — are largely indifferent to this problem. They optimized for standardized products at scale. The B2B world, where products resist standardization by nature, was left to fend for itself.

The optimistic reading: AI might close this gap. Semantic matching across vocabularies, automated spec evaluation, regulatory mapping across jurisdictions. These are all solvable problems.

The pessimistic reading: complexity outpaces adaptation, and market access concentrates among firms large enough to bear the costs — while an ever-growing population of viable products never finds its market.
