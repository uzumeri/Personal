Two aerial bombardment campaigns are underway right now. Both rest on the same bet: that enough punishment from the sky will force a country to submit.

History has tested this bet at least a dozen times since 1940. The scorecard is not ambiguous.

The Blitz didn't break Britain. 2.7 million tons of Allied bombs didn't break Germany — ground forces had to physically occupy the country. The firebombing of 67 Japanese cities didn't produce surrender. Korea absorbed more bomb tonnage than the entire Pacific theater of WWII and ended on the same lines where it started. Rolling Thunder dropped 864,000 tons on North Vietnam over three years. Vietnam won.

The pattern is so consistent that Robert Pape, in his landmark study Bombing to Win, reached a stark conclusion: punishment campaigns aimed at breaking civilian morale almost never succeed.

So why does the doctrine keep getting tried?

Because strategic bombing is a disease reserved for truly powerful militaries. Only a handful of nations possess the air forces and logistics to sustain a long-range bombardment campaign. For those that do, the capability sits right there — visible, available, seductive. It promises results without body bags, without occupation, without the political cost of a ground war. It looks like an easy way out.

And sometimes a country is dumb enough to take it.

This isn't stupidity in the conventional sense. It's the institutional pathology of the strong. When you have an overwhelming advantage in one dimension of military power, it warps your strategic thinking. Every problem starts to look like a target set. The generals advocate for the tools they command. The politicians want to "do something" without committing ground troops. The intelligence community produces target lists because that's what it's organized to produce.

The result: a great power reaches for the tool it's best at using, rather than the tool the situation actually requires.

But there's a deeper reason it fails. When the aggressor's demands represent an existential threat — territorial dismemberment, regime destruction, forced capitulation — surrender is not a viable option. A population facing national extinction will absorb enormous punishment rather than accept terms that amount to death.

Russia's demands on Ukraine amount to vassal status. The US/Israeli demands on Iran require strategic concessions no regime has ever made under bombardment alone. The historical record is unambiguous about what happens next.

The doctrine fails not because the bombs are insufficiently powerful, but because the theory of how human societies respond to existential coercion is wrong. Douhet published that theory in 1921. It's 2026. A century of evidence says the same thing.

The question is whether anyone in a position of authority has read the scorecard.
