Everybody saw the tariffs. Everybody saw the sanctions. The Strait of Hormuz closing was on every screen for a week.

What almost nobody sees is what happens next — because what happens next takes months.

In operations management, there is a concept called the feedback time constant. It is the delay between a shock to a system and the moment when the consequences become visible. In a short supply chain, the delay might be days. In a global supply chain — raw materials crossing three continents, six ports, a dozen intermediaries — the time constant is weeks or months.

This delay is the most dangerous feature of the current crisis.

There is a theorem called Little's Law. It says: Inventory = Throughput × Flow Time. When you shut off a supply (sanctions, tariffs, a closed strait), the pipeline doesn't empty immediately. The oil already in tankers still arrives. The goods already in warehouses still sell at the old price. The system looks fine — for now.

But buffers deplete. Little's Law is patient and arithmetic. When the pipeline inventory is consumed and nothing replaces it, the effects arrive not as a gradual adjustment but as a cliff.

We saw this with COVID. The shocks of early 2020 didn't fully manifest until late 2020 and 2021. By the time the chip shortage hit auto manufacturing, most people had forgotten a virus in Wuhan had anything to do with it. The time constant was nine to twelve months.

The same physics governs 2026. Sanctions on Russian energy. Sanctions on Iranian oil. Tariffs on Chinese manufacturing. Closure of the Strait of Hormuz. Each is a sledgehammer blow to the input end of supply chains that feed billions of people.

The people who understand time constants — commodity traders, logistics planners, supply chain professionals — are already watching the dashboards. They can see buffer levels declining and shipping patterns rerouting.

But these people do not set national policy. The people who set national policy are operating on feedback loops measured in news cycles — hours and days, not weeks and months. They are structurally incapable of seeing the slow wreck they are engineering, because it hasn't happened yet. It is still in the pipeline.

What concerns me most is that multiple massive disruptions are being imposed simultaneously. Each might be absorbable alone. But sanctions plus tariffs plus a closed strait plus a land war in Europe is not a linear sum. The buffers that absorb one shock are the same buffers needed to absorb the next.

Little's Law doesn't care about politics. It is arithmetic. And the arithmetic, right now, is very bad.
