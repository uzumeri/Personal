The United States is currently asking for help to secure the Strait of Hormuz—the vital waterway through which twenty percent of global oil flows. As the conflict with Iran escalates, the administration needs warships to protect commercial vessels.

They have asked NATO members. They have asked Japan, South Korea, Australia, the UK, and others. And the answer, almost universally, has been no. 

These are not neutral countries. These are the closest, oldest, most tightly integrated allies the US has ever had. In any previous decade, they would have answered the call instantly. Today, they are flatly refusing.

Why? Because Donald Trump has spent the last twelve months systematically breaking the single most valuable asset the United States possesses on the global stage: trust.

The US spent 70 years building a global alliance network based on trust—and dismantled it in 12 months. It hasn't taken long for the chickens to come home to roost. 

I have worked in business for nearly 50 years, and I know that trust is infrastructure. You can build a technically perfect product or marketplace, but if participants don't trust the system, they don't participate. And if trust is broken, they don't come back—they build their own systems to route around you.

That's exactly what allied nations are doing right now. They aren't walking away because they want to decouple; they are building backups because they have to plan for a world where their primary partner is unpredictable. Decades build a trust reserve, but one betrayal empties it.
