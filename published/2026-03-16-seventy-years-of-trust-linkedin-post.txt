The United States spent seventy years building the most powerful alliance network in human history. In just twelve months, the foundation of that network—trust—has been dismantled.

If you spend thirty years building a business and then hand the keys to someone who insults suppliers and breaks contracts, the buildings might still stand, but the business is dying. The same applies to international relations. Treaties and bases are the mechanics, but the real glue has always been trust. 

It hasn't taken long for the chickens to come home to roost. Donald Trump is currently asking former allies for help to re-open the Strait of Hormuz, even as his administration continues to fuel the crisis and Iranian resistance by bombing Iran on a daily basis. 

So far, all of those prior allies are refusing. In the past, they would have stepped up to aid the US. But now that trust has been systematically betrayed across so many issues, no one is willing to extend the United States credit. The bill for a transactional foreign policy is coming due.

I have worked in business for nearly 50 years, and I know that trust is infrastructure. You can build a technically perfect product or marketplace, but if participants don't trust the system, they don't participate. And if trust is broken, they don't come back—they build their own systems to route around you.

That's exactly what allied nations are doing right now. They aren't walking away because they want to decouple; they are building backups because they have to plan for a world where their primary partner is unpredictable. Decades build a trust reserve, but one betrayal empties it.
