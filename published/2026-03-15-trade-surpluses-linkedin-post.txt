Producing more goods than you can domestically consume isn't a "market abuse." It is the literal definition of trade.

Yet under its new U.S. Trade Representative, the administration has announced sweeping investigations into 16 major economies, using exactly that justification. 

In a recent press release, USTR Jamieson Greer claimed that our trading partners are guilty of "structural excess capacity" because they are producing more than they consume. Those "persistent trade surpluses," the USTR argues, are hurting American manufacturing.

Take a moment to absorb how profoundly flawed that economic logic is.

If you are a farmer who grows wheat, you generate a massive, persistent "wheat surplus." You then trade that surplus for things you can't produce yourself, like a tractor. Your surplus isn't an abusive act—it is the very engine of the global economy. 

Nobody trades on a perfect barter system. We use currency specifically because surpluses and deficits are a natural, permanent part of any market. 

There are genuine trade abuses in the world, like state-sponsored dumping or IP theft, that absolutely demand a rigorous response. But conflating those real issues with the simple existence of a trade surplus criminalizes the very concept of comparative advantage.

When a policy rationale contradicts thousands of years of human exchange, you have to ask why. Are the architects of this policy really that economically clueless? Did they rush out a legally shaky justification in a panic? Or is this trade conflict an intentional distraction from much larger geopolitical crises, launched like a massive Friday news dump?

Whatever the reason, the path toward a "perfectly balanced" trade world is the path toward autarky. And historically, that's where innovation stalls and global wealth collapses.
