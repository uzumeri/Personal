Two bombing campaigns are underway right now, both resting on the same century-old bet: that enough punishment from the sky will force a country to submit.

I went through the historical scorecard — the Blitz, the Allied bombing of Germany and Japan, Korea, Vietnam, Kosovo, Yemen — and the record is remarkably clear. Punishment campaigns aimed at breaking civilian morale almost never work. When the demands represent an existential threat to the country being bombed, the probability drops to near zero.

The doctrine fails not because the bombs aren't powerful enough, but because the theory of how societies respond to existential coercion is wrong.
