# you asked

In the current events surrounding the US and Israeli attacks on Iran, most of the criticism has been directed at Donald Trump. However, Benjamin Netanyahu is apparently an active co-conspirator in the decision-making. 

Is there any credible reporting to tell us what his role was in the decision. Did he convince Donald Trump or vice-versa? 

Now that global dismay is building over the effects of the war, I sense that Netanyahu is being relatively quiet, possibly so he can expand Israel's attacks on Hezbollah and the Palestinians with less global scrutiny.

What reporting or evidence exists to confirm, deny or adjust my impression?

---

# perplexity response

There is credible reporting that Netanyahu actively pushed for the strikes and helped shape the timing and scale, but public evidence does not let us say with confidence whether he “convinced” Trump in a simple, one‑way sense. The picture that emerges is of a joint project in which Netanyahu persistently pressed for war aims he has long favored, while Trump controlled the terms and scope of U.S. involvement.

## What we know about Netanyahu’s role

- Multiple reports describe Netanyahu as a central driver of the idea of striking Iran, well before the actual attack.  
  - Axios reports that Netanyahu personally called Trump with detailed intelligence about a rare gathering of Iran’s supreme leader and top advisers in Tehran and framed it as a unique opportunity for a decapitation strike, after which the joint U.S.–Israeli attack followed within hours.[[0]](https://www.axios.com/2026/03/03/trump-netanyahu-call-iran-war-israel-coordination)
  - A South Korean outlet, summarizing U.S. and Israeli reporting including the New York Times, says Netanyahu had already requested Trump’s authorization for strikes on Iranian missile bases during a December meeting at Mar‑a‑Lago, and later held a three‑hour White House session in February to discuss concrete attack dates and war scenarios.[[1]](https://www.chosun.com/english/world-en/2026/03/04/7BKJZLXEUZCUHAY2TLR46EDBCM/)

- That same reporting describes U.S. officials characterizing the decision to attack as heavily influenced by Netanyahu’s persistence.  
  - The Chosun Ilbo account, based on U.S. and foreign media plus statements from Trump’s team, concludes that “the U.S. decision to strike Iran was Netanyahu’s victory,” explicitly tying the outcome to sustained Israeli pressure and internal debates in Washington.[[1]](https://www.chosun.com/english/world-en/2026/03/04/7BKJZLXEUZCUHAY2TLR46EDBCM/)

- Netanyahu himself has publicly linked the campaign to his long‑standing aims.  
  - In early March, he released a video saying the operations against Iran were being carried out with the help of his “friend” Donald Trump and adding that this coalition allowed him to pursue what he had “wanted for 40 years,” i.e., a major confrontation designed to roll back Iran’s power.[[2]](https://www.aljazeera.com/news/2026/3/2/rubio-suggests-timing-of-us-strikes-on-iran-was-influenced-by-israeli-plans)

Taken together, this supports the view that Netanyahu was more than a passive partner: he repeatedly pushed for action, framed intelligence to underscore urgency, and saw the war as the culmination of a decades‑long agenda.

## Who convinced whom?

Open sources suggest a mutual reinforcement dynamic, not a clean story of one man convincing a reluctant other.

- On one side, Trump is portrayed as receptive but also running a parallel track of diplomacy:  
  - Axios reports that while Netanyahu urged seizing the Tehran leadership meeting as a target, part of Trump’s team was still working diplomatic options, and one U.S. official described the White House as “negotiating” with one hand and doing joint military planning with Israel with the other, with Trump “constantly weighing both.”[[0]](https://www.axios.com/2026/03/03/trump-netanyahu-call-iran-war-israel-coordination)
  - The Soufan Center notes that even earlier in 2026, Trump had at times preferred diplomacy and sanctions over direct regime‑change war, creating strategic differences with Netanyahu, who supported more aggressive regional military action.[[3]](https://thesoufancenter.org/intelbrief-2026-february-18/)

- On the other side, several accounts highlight that Israeli plans and determination shaped the timing and made U.S. participation feel almost inevitable to some in Washington:  
  - Reporting summarized by Chosun Ilbo quotes U.S. officials and congressional briefings describing Israel as “determined to act,” with the logic that, since the U.S. would be dragged in anyway, pre‑coordination was “rational,” and that Israeli pressure was a key factor behind the final decision.[[1]](https://www.chosun.com/english/world-en/2026/03/04/7BKJZLXEUZCUHAY2TLR46EDBCM/)
  - Members of Congress have reportedly raised questions about being told that Israel would initiate strikes leading to U.S. involvement, with some lawmakers disturbed by the sense that Israeli decisions were effectively setting the pace for Washington.[[4]](https://thehill.com/policy/international/5765600-netanyahu-israel-iran-strikes/)

So far, there is no single, authoritative account (akin to a declassified memo or full investigative reconstruction) definitively saying “Netanyahu convinced Trump” or “Trump pushed Netanyahu.” Instead, the weight of reporting supports this adjustment of your impression: Netanyahu persistently pulled in the direction of confrontation and shaped the options on the table, while Trump chose a version of that confrontation that fit his own political and strategic calculus.

## Different war aims and current behavior

Several analyses argue that Trump and Netanyahu share a willingness to use force but diverge in their end goals, which matters for your sense that Netanyahu is now lying low while expanding other campaigns.

- U.S. objectives vs. Israeli objectives:  
  - A New York Times analysis describes Netanyahu as finally obtaining “the war he always wanted” against Iran but “on Trump’s terms,” noting that Netanyahu has long favored regime change, whereas Trump appears more interested in degrading Iran’s nuclear and missile capabilities and reopening maritime routes rather than a prolonged regime‑change war.[[5]](https://www.nytimes.com/2026/03/14/world/middleeast/netanyahu-iran-war-trump.html)
  - A separate NYT opinion piece and CNN analysis both emphasize that U.S. goals now center on constraining Iran and stabilizing oil flows, while Israel’s leadership has broader ambitions to weaken or topple the regime and roll back its “Axis of Resistance” (Hezbollah, militias, etc.), creating growing divergence as the war drags on.[[6]](https://www.cnn.com/2026/03/16/politics/israel-iran-trump-us-goals-hormuz-nato-analysis)[[7]](https://www.nytimes.com/2026/03/11/opinion/iran-trump-netanyahu-israel.html)

- Netanyahu’s regional agenda and Hezbollah/Palestinians:  
  - Think‑tank analysis from the Soufan Center underscores that even before this war, Netanyahu claimed a right to act militarily across the region—including against Iranian proxies—without needing specific U.S. approval, and U.S. officials felt obliged to factor this into their Iran strategy.[[3]](https://thesoufancenter.org/intelbrief-2026-february-18/)
  - Prior reporting on Gaza and Hezbollah shows a pattern of Netanyahu using regional escalations to pursue wider goals, while accepting international criticism as the cost of pursuing what he presents as Israel’s security imperatives.[[8]](https://www.bbc.com/news/articles/cpqv2qjg5vvo)[[9]](https://www.newarab.com/news/how-countries-are-responding-israel-hezbollah-ceasefire)

This supports the idea that Netanyahu views the Iran war as part of a broader campaign against Iran’s regional network, not just a discrete operation. It also means that as global outrage focuses on the Iran strikes and oil disruption, he has incentives to advance long‑standing objectives against Hezbollah and Palestinian groups under that wider war umbrella, even if he softens his public tone.

## Has Netanyahu gone quiet?

Your sense that Netanyahu is quieter now compared with earlier phases is partly borne out, but needs nuance.

- Immediately around the strikes and in early March, Netanyahu was not quiet at all: he publicly embraced the joint operation, highlighted his personal role and his relationship with Trump, and framed the war as something he had sought for decades.[[2]](https://www.aljazeera.com/news/2026/3/2/rubio-suggests-timing-of-us-strikes-on-iran-was-influenced-by-israeli-plans)
- More recent coverage from major outlets has indeed focused more on Trump’s speeches, domestic U.S. politics, and global economic fallout, with Netanyahu’s statements mostly appearing in shorter segments about Israeli tactics in Lebanon and Gaza rather than as the main narrative.[[10]](https://www.nytimes.com/live/2026/03/16/world/iran-war-trump-oil-lebanon)[[11]](https://www.bbc.com/news/articles/cgk2lm5znyro)[[6]](https://www.cnn.com/2026/03/16/politics/israel-iran-trump-us-goals-hormuz-nato-analysis)
- Analysts note that as U.S.–Israeli objectives diverge, both leaders are adjusting their messaging, with Trump foregrounding “limited” goals and burden‑sharing, and Netanyahu emphasizing Israel’s right to continue operations against Iran’s allies while saying less about ultimate end‑states in public.[[7]](https://www.nytimes.com/2026/03/11/opinion/iran-trump-netanyahu-israel.html)[[6]](https://www.cnn.com/2026/03/16/politics/israel-iran-trump-us-goals-hormuz-nato-analysis)[[3]](https://thesoufancenter.org/intelbrief-2026-february-18/)

So there is some basis for updating your impression like this: Netanyahu was highly visible and assertive in pushing for and justifying the initial Iran strikes, but as the costs and backlash mount—and as Israeli and U.S. goals diverge—he appears to be calibrating his public tone while continuing or even expanding operations against Hezbollah and in the Palestinian arena within the broader war frame.

## How your impression holds up

Putting this together:

- There is credible evidence that Netanyahu was an **active initiator and accelerator** of the decision to attack Iran, repeatedly pressing Trump and U.S. officials, presenting time‑sensitive intelligence, and lobbying for a broader confrontation.[[5]](https://www.nytimes.com/2026/03/14/world/middleeast/netanyahu-iran-war-trump.html)[[0]](https://www.axios.com/2026/03/03/trump-netanyahu-call-iran-war-israel-coordination)[[2]](https://www.aljazeera.com/news/2026/3/2/rubio-suggests-timing-of-us-strikes-on-iran-was-influenced-by-israeli-plans)[[1]](https://www.chosun.com/english/world-en/2026/03/04/7BKJZLXEUZCUHAY2TLR46EDBCM/)
