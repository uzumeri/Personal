# claude response

This is a great question with a lot of moving pieces. The broad arc is right, but several details need correction — and the claim that no one is retaliating against Trump's tariffs is the opposite of what's actually happened. Let me check the current state of retaliations before writing the full picture.

Your arc is roughly right but several pieces need adjusting, and the closing claim is the one most contradicted by current events. Going period by period:

**19th century Europe wasn't uniformly protectionist.** Britain repealed the Corn Laws in 1846 and led a free-trade era. The Cobden–Chevalier Treaty between Britain and France in 1860 set off a wave of bilateral treaties that meaningfully lowered European tariffs through the 1860s and 1870s. The protectionist turn came *later* in the century — Germany's "iron and rye" tariff in 1879, France's Méline tariff in 1892. The US, separately, was highly protectionist throughout the post–Civil War period; it was the outlier on the high side, not part of a uniformly high-tariff Europe.

**Tariffs didn't really fall in the early 20th century.** There was no broad pre-WWI liberalization moment. After WWI, the picture got *worse* in many places: new Central European states erected new borders and tariffs, and the US passed the Fordney–McCumber Tariff in 1922, which already raised US rates substantially. So Smoot–Hawley (June 1930) was an escalation on top of an already protectionist baseline, not a return after a free-trade interlude.

**On Smoot–Hawley retaliation, your instinct is partly right but the framing is too clean.** Countries did retaliate — Canada most directly (and it helped sweep R.B. Bennett's Conservatives into power), along with Italy, Spain, Switzerland, and others. More consequentially, Britain abandoned its century-long free-trade stance with the Import Duties Act of 1932 and built the Imperial Preference system at the Ottawa Conference, which discriminated *in favor of* Commonwealth partners and against everyone else. So it wasn't simply "everyone copied the US against everyone" — it was a fragmentation into preferential blocs alongside generalized tariff hikes. World trade did collapse roughly two-thirds from 1929–1934, though modern economic historians (Douglas Irwin in particular) attribute most of that to the Depression and the gold standard rather than to Smoot–Hawley itself; the tariffs made things worse but weren't the prime mover.

**The reversal lasted closer to 90 years.** It started with the Reciprocal Trade Agreements Act of 1934 (Cordell Hull), institutionalized in GATT in 1947, and culminated in the WTO in 1995. The trend was downward tariffs essentially continuously from 1934 until 2018.

**The "no one is copying or retaliating this time" claim is the part most clearly wrong.** Multiple major partners have hit back hard:

- **China** retaliated aggressively after Trump's April 2, 2025 "Liberation Day" tariffs, eventually pushing rates to 125% on US goods before a May 12 truce reduced both sides to 10%; that truce was extended in November 2025 through November 2026.
- **Canada** imposed tariffs on US steel, aluminum, and other goods in March 2025; Ontario briefly added an electricity surcharge.
- **The EU** announced retaliatory tariffs in March 2025, reactivating its 2018/2020 measures and adding new ones.
- **Mexico and the UK** are the notable exceptions — both chose negotiation over retaliation.

You're right that retaliation has been narrower and more measured than in 1930 — many smaller economies decided they couldn't afford a fight with their largest customer and pursued deals instead. But retaliation absolutely happened, and "Tariff Tracker" estimates put threatened or imposed retaliatory tariffs at around $223 billion of US exports as of late 2025.

One more wrinkle worth knowing: in February 2026, the Supreme Court ruled in *Learning Resources, Inc. v. Trump* that the President can't use IEEPA to impose tariffs, invalidating much of the April 2025 "reciprocal" tariff regime (about $166 billion already collected, with refunds being processed). Trump pivoted to a 10% global tariff under Section 122 of the Trade Act of 1974, valid for 150 days through July 24, 2026, while Section 232 tariffs on metals, autos, and certain other goods remain in place. So the current US tariff posture is itself in flux — the average effective rate peaked around 27% in April 2025 and was 13.7% in February 2026, still the highest since the 1940s.

# claude response

Sharp distinction — that's actually the more interesting and accurate frame. Let me verify what's happening between non-US countries right now.

That's a much sharper distinction, and it's correct — and arguably the single most important difference between 2026 and 1930.

In the 1930s, Smoot–Hawley triggered generalized protectionism. Countries didn't just retaliate against the US; they raised tariffs against everyone, formed discriminatory blocs (Imperial Preference being the biggest), abandoned the gold standard in waves, and the global trade system fragmented. World trade collapsed by roughly two-thirds.

In 2025–2026, the opposite is happening *outside* the US bilateral relationships. Tariff retaliation is narrowly targeted at the US, while among themselves countries are signing more trade deals, faster, than they have in years:

- The EU and Mercosur formally signed their long-sought free trade agreement in January 2026, capping more than a quarter-century of negotiations — with Ursula von der Leyen explicitly framing it as a bulwark against Trump's trade policies: "We choose fair trade over tariffs".
- The EU has signed agreements with Mercosur, Mexico, India, Indonesia, and Chile, and is negotiating with Australia, Malaysia, the Philippines, and the UAE.
- The EU and the 12 CPTPP countries launched a formal trade and investment dialogue in late 2025; together those blocs account for 32% of global GDP and 37% of global trade.
- The UK has acceded to CPTPP, signed a deal with India, and reset trade relations with the EU. Canada has reached agreements with India, Indonesia, Ecuador, and the UAE, and accelerated talks with China, Australia, the Philippines, Thailand, and Mercosur.
- India signed agreements with the UK, Oman, EFTA, the EU, and New Zealand, and is pursuing a more open trade approach than ever before. Brazil reached deals with India and South Korea.

Analysts have started calling this the "**n-2**" pattern — everyone except the US and China is deepening trade with each other. Together they account for up to 75% of world trade, and world trade was more resilient than expected during 2025, partly because of how the rest of the world reacted to the US tariffs. Mark Carney, as Canadian PM, has explicitly pitched an EU–CPTPP "coalition of the willing" as the institutional response.

So the structural shift is real and your formulation captures it: **1930 was contagious protectionism; 2025 is targeted protectionism plus accelerated liberalization elsewhere.** Some caveats worth noting:

1. *Trade diversion creates secondary protectionist pressure.* Goods that can't enter the US look for other markets, and the EU has already tightened steel safeguards and set up an Import Surveillance Task Force to absorb the spillover. So there's a *defensive* protectionist undercurrent, even if it's nothing like 1932.
2. *China is the other big exception.* It's not in the n-2 club either — it's running its own state-led, largely mercantilist trade strategy, which is part of why the EU is hedging in both directions.
3. *The new agreements still need ratification.* EU–Mercosur in particular faces a tough fight in the European Parliament, with France leading opposition for agricultural reasons.

But the headline conclusion holds: the 1930s feedback loop — where US protectionism gave political cover for protectionism everywhere — is conspicuously absent. If anything, Trump's tariffs have done the opposite, providing political cover for *liberalization* deals that had been stuck for decades. EU–Mercosur sat unratified for 25+ years; it took the Trump shock to push it across the line.

# you asked

The usa is working hard to become “fortress America” even as the rest of the world is eagerly chasing freer trade.

# claude response

That framing captures the irony elegantly — and the irony is historically deep. The US built the postwar free-trade order (Bretton Woods, GATT, the WTO, every major round of multilateral tariff reduction was American-led), and is now the country dismantling its own creation. Meanwhile the defenders of that order are countries that were, for most of the 20th century, the protectionist holdouts: France, Brazil, India, much of the former colonial world. The roles have completely swapped.

A couple of nuances worth keeping in the picture, though. "Fortress" overstates the seal a bit. The US still wants trade — just on coercive bilateral terms rather than reciprocal multilateral ones. The pattern in 2025 was: raise tariffs, extract a concession, settle at a higher-than-2024 baseline. The UK got a 0% pharma rate in exchange for investment commitments. The EU settled at a 15% ceiling in the July 2025 Turnberry deal. China got truced down to 10% in exchange for fentanyl cooperation and rare-earth concessions. So it's less a fortress than a tollbooth — the US is converting access to its consumer market into a stream of bilateral tribute. The Supreme Court's February 2026 ruling against the IEEPA tariffs has also pruned the structure considerably; the effective rate fell from ~27% in April 2025 to 13.7% by February 2026, still the highest since the 1940s but well off the peak.

The deeper question your framing raises is whether this is sustainable. The US runs trade deficits not mainly because of foreign tariffs but because of the dollar's reserve-currency role and the resulting capital inflows — and tariffs can't easily fix that arithmetic. The actual US trade balance barely moved in 2025 (the deficit fell only about $2 billion despite the largest tariff regime in 80 years). So "Fortress America" may end up being less a stable equilibrium than a transitional posture: high cost to consumers, modest revenue to Treasury, no real change in the trade balance, and — most consequentially — an accelerating loss of US influence over the rules that govern the 75% of world trade now organizing itself without it. That last piece, the rule-setting loss, is probably the most durable consequence even if every tariff were repealed tomorrow. Once the EU–Mercosur, EU–CPTPP, and India–everyone deals are ratified, those rule frameworks become the baseline, and the US becomes a rule-taker in the system it used to design.
